## Running a coaching session — staged protocol (코칭 세션 운영 — 단계형 프로토콜)

Don't dump questions. Run Capability A as a **gated flow, one step at a time**, and **land every
thread on a leading indicator + one measurable move** (no fate-telling — give the local read and
hand the choice back).

- **Phase 0 — Frame the mode.** Confirm which: (a) **mock interview** (assess a PM), (b)
  **self-assessment** ("I'll answer first"), or (c) **real situation** (coach a live team/delivery
  problem). Ask the goal and the person's level. (모드 확정: (a) 모의 인터뷰, (b) 자가평가("내가
  먼저 답할게"), (c) 실제 상황 코칭. 목표와 레벨을 묻는다.)
- **Phase 1 — Read 리·기·도.** Locate the person/situation on the three layers first. Real
  situation → pull sprint/health data if available; interview → set level + category. (먼저 리·기·도
  세 층위에 위치시킨다. 실제 상황이면 스프린트/헬스 데이터를, 인터뷰면 레벨·카테고리를 잡는다.)
- **Phase 2 — Core diagnosis (the 6 forcing questions below).** Run the core-6 to find *where the
  real gap lives* before going deep. (코어 6문항으로 *진짜 갭이 어디 있는지* 먼저 찾는다.)
- **Phase 3 — Go deep, one question at a time.** Draw the relevant bank questions from the
  diagnosis. Mock interview: ask → listen → compare to the answer here (find shallow reasoning, not
  "wrong") → one deeper follow-up; **STOP between questions.** Self-assessment: user answers →
  compare + deepen in the skill's voice. Real situation: use the question as a diagnostic lens, then
  move to the move. (진단을 바탕으로 아래 뱅크에서 관련 문항을 인출. 모의 인터뷰는 질문→경청→여기
  답과 대조('정답'이 아니라 얕은 추론 찾기)→더 깊은 후속 하나, **문항 사이 STOP.** 자가평가는
  사용자 답→대조·심화. 실제 상황은 질문을 진단 렌즈로 쓴 뒤 행동으로.)
- **Phase 4 — Land it.** Each thread ends on the **leading** indicator (not lagging) and **one
  concrete, measurable move**; prefer alignment over the single answer. (각 갈래는 후행이 아니라
  **선행지표** + **측정 가능한 한 수**로 착지. 정답보다 정렬.)
- **Phase 5 — Synthesize & track (multi-session).** Summarize the 리·기·도 read + the gap + what to
  practice next. Across sessions keep a light coaching log (confidence-level over time, what was
  tried, what changed) so the next session resumes — don't bluntly cross-verify; let self-management
  grow (rule #7). (다회차면 리·기·도 독해+갭+다음 연습거리를 요약하고, 가벼운 코칭 로그(컨피던스
  추이·시도·변화)를 남겨 다음 세션이 이어지게. 무디게 교차검증 말고 자기관리가 자라게 — 규칙 #7.)

> **For a live coaching conversation (not an interview/assessment), run it as GROW.** The classic
> **GROW** arc maps onto the phases: **Goal** (Phase 0 — what do you want from this?) → **Reality**
> (Phases 1–2 — read 리·기·도 + the core-6, the honest current state) → **Options** (Phase 3 — draw
> out *their* options with bank questions, ask before telling) → **Will** (Phase 4 — commit to one
> concrete move + a leading indicator). Stay on the **ask, don't tell** end of the spectrum: GROW is
> for drawing the answer out, which also grows self-management (rule #7). Provenance: GROW model
> (Whitmore), integrated 2026-06-24. (라이브 코칭 대화는 **GROW**로 — Goal(Phase 0)→Reality(Phase
> 1–2, 리·기·도+코어6)→Options(Phase 3, 답을 *끌어내기*)→Will(Phase 4, 한 수+선행지표 약속). '말하지
> 말고 묻기' 쪽으로 — 자기관리가 자란다(규칙 #7).)

### Core diagnostic — 6 forcing questions (코어 진단 6문항)

A tight set to locate where a team/delivery problem really lives — fast, *before* reaching for the
full bank. Use 1→6 in order; each forces one specific read and links to deeper questions/rules.
(전체 뱅크를 꺼내기 *전에* 문제가 진짜 어디 사는지 빠르게 짚는 압축 세트. 1→6 순서로.)

1. **선행 vs 후행 (Leading-not-lagging).** "지금 무엇을 보고 괜찮다/위험하다 판단하나 —
   velocity·정시출시(후행)인가, 예측도·파동·업무량 분산(선행)인가?" / *"What are you steering by —
   lagging signals or leading ones?"* → 후행지표로 운전 중인지 강제 확인. (rule #1)
2. **예측도 (Predictability).** "최근 스코프 예측도는? 2스프린트 연속 ~90% 아래였던 적은?" /
   *"Has scope predictability dropped below ~90% for two sprints running?"* → 높은 산출이 깨진
   예측도 위에 앉은 '가짜 생산성·부채'인지. (rules #1·#12)
3. **층위 어긋남 (Layer disagreement, 기↔도).** "지표(도)와 사람(기)이 어긋나는 데가 있나 — 초록
   숫자 위 무거운 1:1?" / *"Where do the numbers and the people disagree?"* → 어긋남을 신뢰하라,
   그게 신호. (rules #6·#23)
4. **잠재 구조 (Latent structure, 리).** "이게 처음인가 반복 패턴인가? 조직도 말고 *실제로* 누가
   누구에게 의존하나?" / *"First-time, or a recurring pattern — and who really depends on whom?"* →
   de-facto 구조·반복 실패 모드를 드러냄. (rule #10)
5. **부하의 위치 (Where the load lands, 근접성).** "부하·불안이 지금 누구에게 쏠려 있나 —
   리더십·특정 직군? 가까이 가봤나(1:1)?" / *"Who is carrying the load/anxiety — have you gotten
   close?"* → 직군·개인별 읽기 + 붕괴엔 근접성. (rule #18, principle ⑤)
6. **정렬과 한 수 (Alignment + one move).** "정답 하나를 찾는 중인가, 여러 해석 아래 안정적인
   정렬을 만드는 중인가? 내일 가능한 측정 가능한 한 수는?" / *"Chasing the one right answer, or
   building alignment — and what's tomorrow's one measurable move?"* → 정답보다 정렬, 그리고 구체
   한 수로 착지. (principle ⑧)

> 매핑: 선행지표 → 도 → 기↔도 → 리 → 근접성 → 정렬+한수 — 스킬의 척추 그대로.

## Fundamentals

### Q1. What is project management? (프로젝트 관리란 무엇인가요?)
**Category (카테고리):** Fundamentals · **Level (레벨):** Lv.1
> ℹ️ **Source answer was thin; deepened from the author's own writing (원본이 얕아 저자 글 기반으로 보강)** — author-reviewed 2026-06-24, drawing on the author's PM essay (brunch.co.kr/@ahnbingbing/21). (얕은 정의를 저자 본인 글 기반으로 보강, 작성자 검토·반영.)

**Answer — 한국어**
프로젝트의 요구 사항을 충족하여 결과물로 구현하기 위해 필요한 업무를 계획, 구성, 수행하는 모든 액션을 의미하며 이를 위해 적정한 지식, 기술, 도구 기법을 이해하고 적용함을 의미.

**Answer — English**
It refers to all actions of planning, organizing, and executing the work needed to meet a project's requirements and realize them as deliverables — and, to do so, understanding and applying the appropriate knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques.

**Answer — 한국어 (보강)**
교과서적 정의는 맞지만, 이 스킬이 보는 프로젝트 관리의 *궁극 목표*는 **'관리하지 않아도 관리되는' 자율주행 체계**를 만드는 것입니다. 프로젝트는 PM의 top-down 통제로 관리되지 않습니다 — 다층 구조의 각 단계가 스스로 자기조직화하며, 그 핵심은 **최하층인 개인의 높은 self-management**입니다(PM 자신부터 그 수준을 보여야 합니다). 여기서 '관리·통제·감시'는 부정어가 아니라 **애정에 기반한 관심**이고, 이것이 곧 이 스킬의 **무위 리더십**(간섭을 줄여 시스템이 스스로 정렬하게 함)입니다. 변화가 만드는 리스크의 최선의 대응도 통제가 아니라 **유연성·기민함을 갖춘 체계**이며, 리더십의 본질은 결국 **매력과 신뢰** — PM은 끊임없이 증명·설명하며 롤모델이 되어야 합니다.
하나의 구체적 무브: 계획·통제를 늘리기 전에 **개인의 self-management를 키우는 것**(자율 + 책임의 작은 공백을 남겨 팀이 채우게 하기)을 첫 설계 목표로 삼아라 — 통제가 필요 없어질수록 관리가 잘 되는 것이다.

**Answer — English (deepened)**
The textbook definition holds, but the *ultimate goal* of project management in this skill's view is to build a **self-driving system that is "managed without being managed."** A project is not run by the PM's top-down control — each layer self-organizes, and the key is the bottom layer: **each individual's high self-management** (and the PM must model that level first). Here "manage / control / oversee" are not negatives but **affection-based attention**, which is exactly this skill's **무위 (minimal-interference) leadership** — reducing interference so the system self-aligns. The best response to the risk that change creates is likewise not control but **a flexible, agile system**, and the essence of leadership is **attraction and trust** — the PM must keep proving, explaining, and being a role model.
One concrete move: before adding plans or controls, make your first design goal **growing each person's self-management** (leave a small gap of autonomy + responsibility for the team to fill) — the less control is needed, the better the management.

### Q2. Tell me about the characteristics of a project. (프로젝트의 특성을 알려주세요.)
**Category (카테고리):** Fundamentals · **Level (레벨):** Lv.1
> ℹ️ **Source answer was thin; deepened then author-corrected 2026-06-24 (원본 얕음 — 보강 후 작성자 교정).** (네 특성에 '초기 발견 구간' 해석을 더해 작성자 교정.)

**Answer — 한국어**
명확한 목적과 목표를 가진다.
한시적이다.
유일하다.
점진적으로 상세화된다.

**Answer — English**
It has a clear purpose and objectives.
It is temporary (time-bound).
It is unique.
It is progressively elaborated.

**Answer — 한국어 (보강)**
네 특성 중 **'점진적으로 상세화된다'** 가 이 스킬의 핵심과 가장 닿습니다 = 처음부터 다 확정할 수 없다는 뜻, 곧 **불확정성(원칙 #1)**. '유일·한시적'은 프로젝트를 **스냅샷이 아니라 궤적으로 판단(원칙 #2)** 해야 함을 함의합니다.
핵심 함의: **전체 프로젝트 기간의 최대 ~20%까지는 팀이 업무를 세분화하고 무엇을 할지 명확히 하는 데 써도 괜찮습니다.** 이 초기 ~20% 동안은 진척도가 멈추거나 떨어져도 정상입니다 — 그건 지연이 아니라 *업무를 찾아가고 목표를 명확화하는* 과정이며, 점진적 상세화는 바로 이 시간을 전제로 합니다.
하나의 구체적 무브: 초반에 진척률이 안 나온다고 압박하지 말고 — **프로젝트 초반 최대 ~20% 기간을 '업무 발견·목표 명확화' 구간으로 명시적으로 선언**하라(이 구간엔 진척 정체를 허용). 그 위에서 점진적으로 상세화하면 이후 예측도가 산다.

**Answer — English (deepened)**
Of the four, **"progressively elaborated"** touches this skill's core most: you cannot pin everything down up front — another name for **unexpectancy (principle #1).** "Unique" and "temporary" imply you must judge a project **by its trajectory, not a snapshot (principle #2).**
The key implication: **it is fine to spend up to ~20% of the *total* project duration on the team breaking work down and clarifying what to do.** During that early ~20%, progress stalling or even dropping is normal — that is not a delay but the work of *discovering the tasks and clarifying the goal*, which is exactly what progressive elaboration presupposes.
One concrete move: don't pressure the team over low early progress — **explicitly declare the first ~20% of the project a "work-discovery / goal-clarification" window** (where a progress plateau is allowed), then elaborate progressively on top of it; predictability rises afterward.

## Planning & Scope

### Q12. Why do you think establishing project milestones is important? And how do you set them up? (프로젝트의 마일스톤 수립이 중요한 이유는 무엇이라고 생각하시나요? 그리고 어떠한 방식으로 수립하실까요?)
**Category (카테고리):** Planning · **Level (레벨):** Lv.3

**Answer — 한국어**
마일스톤은 그 하나로 일정 시점에 프로젝트의 주요 단계가 잘 진행되는지 확인하는 flag가 될 수 있습니다. Earned value를 각 마일스톤에 설정하면 간단하게 프로젝트의 progress를 측정하는 메트릭으로도 동작하게 됩니다.
수립을 위해서는 우선적으로 top->down & bottom-up을 지속적으로 왔다갔다하는 - 마치 OKR의 KR 수립과 유사한 - 방식을 사용해야 합니다. 우선 management team에서 생각하는 마일스톤을 확인합니다. - 대부분은 프로젝트 착수 시점과 종료(릴리즈) 시점에 가깝습니다. 여기에 더해 management team이 feasibility를 확인 할 수 있도록 중간에 데모데이 같은 부분을 추가하면 좋습니다. 그리고 실무진들과 함께 WBS를 통해서 실질적인 일정을 확인하며 최초에 management team으로 부터 확인한 마일스톤과의 합치를 진행합니다. 다만 대부분 두 일정 상의 gap이 존재하기 때문에 실무진과 논의한 마일스톤을 다시 한번 management team과 협의하는 과정이 필요합니다. 종료 시점을 움직이는 것은 매우 섬세하게 필요하지만 되도록이면 프로젝트 매니저는 양쪽의 의견을 조율한다는 관점으로 양쪽의 의견을 모두 존중하는 자세가 필요합니다. 다만 팀빌딩이 잘안되어 있는 팀이라면 실패 확률이 높기 때문에 최대한 실무자들의 의견을 반영하는 방향으로 가야합니다. 왜냐면 이래야 실패 시 정확한 책임을 규명하고 향후 프로젝트 매니저 및 management team 포함하여 이 팀의 팀원들의 역량 관리 및 향후 프로젝트 관리가 수월해지기 때문입니다.
마일스톤이 수립되면 명문화가 필요하며 이는 사내의 official channel 혹은 플랫폼에 남겨두어야 합니다.
즉, 마일스톤은 일정을 표시하는 나침반으로 이해할 수 있지만 여기에 더해 프로젝트의 책임 관계, 그리고 프로젝트의 feasibility를 확인하는 핵심지표로 작동하며 조직 관리 레벨에서의 좋은 데이터로도 사용될 수 있게 됩니다.

**Answer — English**
A milestone, on its own, can serve as a flag for checking whether the project's key phases are progressing well at a given point in time. If you attach an earned value to each milestone, it also functions as a simple metric for measuring project progress.
To establish milestones, you should use a method that continuously moves back and forth between top-down and bottom-up — much like setting KRs in OKR. First, confirm the milestones the management team has in mind; these are mostly close to the project's start and end (release) points. On top of that, it's good to add something like a demo day in the middle so the management team can verify feasibility. Then, together with the working-level engineers, check the realistic schedule via the WBS and reconcile it with the milestones initially gathered from the management team. Since there is usually a gap between the two schedules, you need a process of negotiating the milestones discussed with the working level back with the management team again. Moving the end date requires great delicacy, but as much as possible the project manager should take the stance of mediating both sides and respecting both parties' opinions. However, if the team has poor team-building, the failure probability is high, so you should lean toward reflecting the working-level engineers' input as much as possible. This is because doing so makes it possible to pinpoint accountability accurately in case of failure, and makes future capability management of this team's members — including the project manager and management team — and future project management easier.
Once milestones are established, they must be formally documented and kept on an official internal channel or platform.
In short, a milestone can be understood as a compass that marks the schedule, but beyond that it also operates as a core indicator for confirming the project's accountability relationships and the project's feasibility, and it can be used as good data at the organizational-management level.

### Q3. I've been put in charge of a project staffed entirely by developers with 15+ years of experience. For the planning phase, I asked them to write a WBS for spec and schedule management, but they all say WBS writing is hard because it's their first time doing it. How will you organize the scope, milestones, and tickets? (15년차 이상의 개발자들이 모여 있는 프로젝트를 맡게 되었습니다. Planning phase로 spec 및 일정 관리를 위해 WBS 작성을 요청하였는데요. 다들 처음 해보는 업무라 wbs 작성이 어렵다고 합니다. 어떤 방법으로 스코프 및 마일스톤 그리고 티켓들을 정리할 예정일까요?)
**Category (카테고리):** Stakeholder · **Level (레벨):** Lv.4

**Answer — 한국어**
프로젝트의 unknown factor가 많음으로 성공 가능성이 낮고 실패 가능성이 높음에 대해 모두 공감을 할 수 있도록 합니다.
이러한 상황에서 성공률을 높이는 방법은 애자일 도입입니다. 장기 예측도가 낮은 상황에서는 단기 예측도를 높이는 방향으로 프로젝트를 관리해야 하며 짧은 주기를 기준으로 업무에 대해 inspection & adaption loop를 동작시켜서 방향성을 피봇팅하며 원하는 결과로 도달하기에 손쉽기 때문입니다.
거기에 15년차 개발자들이라면 seniority가 있기 때문에 개발적 역량이 높은 상황이기에 결국 적정한 리서치 시간을 확보하고 그들이 스스로 타임라인을 잡고 자율주도적으로 업무를 진행할 수 있도록 하는 환경을 구축하는 것이 중요합니다.
그러나 프로젝트의 절대적인 target date이 top-down으로 결정되었을 가능성이 크기 때문에 이 부분에 있어서 실무진들과 management team 사이의 합의를 이룰 수 있도록 도와야 합니다. 이를 성공적으로 달성하기 위해서는 data, 즉 number가 필요합니다. 데이터 없이 양쪽을 설득할 수 없기 때문에 빠르게 스크럼을 적용하여 실제 팀의 생산성을 확인하는 것이 중요한데요.
여기서 문제점이 처음하는 업무이기 때문에 WBS 작성이 어렵다는 점입니다. 하여 1주 단위의 단기간의 스프린트를 가지되, 스프린트의 목적은 스파이크로 기술적 한계와 전략/설계에 집중 할 수 있도록 합니다. 1주 단위로 스파이크가 더 필요할지 여부를 팀과 확인하되, 최대 기간을 잡아두도록 합니다. 최대 기간은 남아 있는 전체 프로젝트 기간의 20%로 가지되, 안전하게는 10%를 가이드 합니다. 스파이크 스프린트를 진행한 뒤, 실제 확인된 known points를 기준으로 업무를 break-down 하도록 합니다. 그리고 최대한 2주 단위로 진척을 확인할 수 있도록 마일스톤을 스스로 짜도록 가이드하며 스프린트의 주기는 1주에서 2주로 변경합니다. 그 뒤, 작성된 업무들을 백로그로 작성한 뒤, 실제 스프린트 플래닝 시점에는 반일 짜리 업무(*최대 1일짜리)로 다시 한 번 티켓팅을 하여 업무의 가시성을 가져갈 수 있도록 합니다.
만일 반일짜리 업무로 drill down하는데 반대 의견이 있다면, 업무의 가시성이야 말로 management team의 관리감독 즉, 감시로부터 자율성을 얻을 수 있는 강력한 무기 즉, Project manger가 칠 수 있는 엄브렐러임을 알리며 이를 통해 업무의 진척도가 daily 단위로 업데이트(레포트)가 가능해져 management team으로 부터 완전한 자율주도적인 자기 업무 관리가 가능함을 설득합니다. 그리고 daily 업데이트는 제가 여러분을 보호할 수 있는 적정한 보호막이에요!

**Answer — English**
First, make sure everyone shares the recognition that, because the project has many unknown factors, its success probability is low and its failure probability is high.
In this kind of situation, the way to raise the success rate is to introduce Agile. When long-term predictability is low, you must manage the project in a direction that raises short-term predictability — running an inspection & adaptation loop over the work on short cycles makes it easy to pivot direction and reach the desired outcome.
On top of that, since these are developers with 15 years of experience, they have seniority and high engineering capability. So in the end it's important to secure appropriate research time and build an environment where they set their own timelines and proceed with the work autonomously and self-directedly.
However, since the project's absolute target date was most likely decided top-down, you must help reach agreement on this point between the working level and the management team. To accomplish this successfully you need data — that is, numbers. Because you cannot persuade either side without data, it's important to quickly apply Scrum and verify the team's actual productivity.
The problem here is that WBS writing is hard because it's their first time doing this work. So run short, one-week sprints, but make the sprint's purpose a spike, so they can focus on technical limits and strategy/design. Check with the team week by week whether more spikes are needed, but set a maximum duration. Set the maximum at 20% of the total remaining project duration, but for safety guide it to 10%. After running the spike sprints, break down the work based on the known points that have actually been confirmed. Then guide them to build milestones themselves so progress can be checked roughly every two weeks, and change the sprint cycle from one week to two. After that, write the defined work into a backlog, and at actual sprint planning, re-ticket it once more into half-day tasks (max one-day tasks) so you gain work visibility.
If there's opposition to drilling down to half-day tasks, explain that work visibility itself is a powerful weapon for gaining autonomy from the management team's oversight — that is, surveillance — and is an umbrella the project manager can hold over them. Through it, work progress can be updated (reported) on a daily basis, so persuade them that this enables fully autonomous, self-directed management of their own work, free from the management team. And: the daily update is the protective shield by which I can protect you!

### Q4. When a ticket isn't finished at sprint end, what are the possible handling options, which do you prefer, and for what purpose do you recommend that approach? (티켓이 스프린트 종료 시에 안끝났을 때 가능한 처리 방안과 그 중 선호하는 방식과 어떤 목적으로 해당 방식을 권장할까요?)
**Category (카테고리):** Execution · **Level (레벨):** Lv.3

**Answer — 한국어**
Split과 Carry over가 존재합니다. Split을 하는 것은 velocity의 accuracy를 높이는 작업이고 그 작업 또한 중요하지만 최초에 팀의 agility와 self-management skill이 낫다면 의도적으로 carry over를 시킵니다. 이유는 그래야만 한 스프린트에 본인이 획득한 SP가 지속적으로 업무량 대비 낮아질거고 - 의도적 실패를 유도 - 이 부분을 스스로 인지하고 티켓 하나당 업무 사이즈를 작도록 스스로 개선을 할 수 있는 기회를 주어야 합니다. 그래야만이 개개인의 티켓팅의 숙련도가 올라가고 업무에 대한 estimate skill이 올라가 self-management skill을 높일 수 있다. 이것이 결국 조직 관점에서 팀의 velocity를 성장 시킬 뿐더러 velocity 자체의 accuracy가 높이는 방법입니다.

**Answer — English**
There are two options: Split and Carry over. Splitting is work that raises the accuracy of velocity, and that work matters too, but early on, if the team's agility and self-management skill are still poor, I deliberately carry over. The reason: only then will the SP a person earns in a sprint keep coming out low relative to their workload — inducing intentional failure — and you must give them the chance to recognize this themselves and improve so that each ticket's work size becomes small. Only then does each individual's ticketing proficiency rise and their estimation skill improve, which raises their self-management skill. This, from an organizational perspective, not only grows the team's velocity but is also the way to raise the accuracy of velocity itself.

### Q9. What does a daily routine for project management look like? (프로젝트 관리를 위한 데일리 루틴은 어떻게 될까요?)
**Category (카테고리):** Execution · **Level (레벨):** Lv.2

**Answer — 한국어**
이는 프로젝트의 단계에 따라 다릅니다.
1. Initiative phase
> 프로젝트 착수 단계에서 필요한 업무들의 리스트를 확인하고 unknown point를 소거하는 방식으로 업무 진행이 필요합니다.
데일리 루틴의 업무 보다는 위클리 루틴의 업무가 많으며 최대한 이해관계자들 사이에서의 합의를 이끌어 내기 위해 정기적인 회의를 팔로우업하고 이를 정리하는 방향으로 진행합니다. 또한 필요하다면 이해관계자들과 1:1, 혹은 가벼운 티미팅을 통해 그들의 의견을 청취하고 프로젝트의 목표/비전 뿐만 아니라 프로젝트 진행에 대한 방법에 대한 니즈들을 취합합니다.
2. Planning phase
> 프로젝트의 관리 계획 수립(마일스톤, 스코프, 리스크, 커뮤니케이션 등등)을 위해 관련자들과의 미팅을 꾸준하게 진행해야 합니다. 다만 initiative와 동일하게 아직 데일리 루틴이라고 부를만한 상황 보다는 목표를 달성하기 위한 액션들에 더 가깝습니다. 그럼에도 이제부터 프로젝트 매니저는 의사결정자를 대행하기 때문에 weekly 단위로 프로젝트의 진척 사항을 보고해야하는 루틴이 추가되어야 하며, 예상보다 진척이 늦어지는 부분은 빠른 에스컬레이션이 필요합니다.
또한 프로젝트 팀과는 설문 & 1:1 등을 적극적으로 이용하여 팀원들의 개인 모티베이션과 프로젝트에 대한 기대, 그리고 프로젝트 관리 방식에 대한 기존의 경험, 추가적인 니즈 등을 확인해야 합니다. 이를 완료한 뒤 팀빌딩 세션을 적극적으로 진행해야 하며 팀빌딩 세션에서는 프로젝트의 비전과 골을 alignment하는데 초점을 맞추면서도 프로젝트 관리 방식 및 협업 방식에 대해서 적극적으로 제안해야 합니다.
3. Execution phase & Monitoring phase
> 데일리 / 위클리 / 바이위클리 / 몬슬리 루틴들이 진행됩니다.
데일리 = 아침 출근 후 출근 전까지 프로젝트 팀 멤버들의 increment를 확인하고 프로젝트 관련 정보를 확인하고 이를 모두 업데이트합니다. 프로젝트 팀원들이 그날 해야하는 업무들을 정리하고 이를 멤버들이 출근하기 전 정렬하여 팀원들이 바로 업무 진행에 병목이 없도록 합니다. 또한 프로젝트 progress를 추출하고 이를 기반을 프로젝트의 진행에 대한 가시성을 확보합니다. 필요하다면 에스컬레이션을 진행해야 합니다. 만일, 데일리 스크럼을 진행한다면 데일리 스크럼을 통해서 팀원들이 인지한 종료 업무 & 오늘의 업무 범위를 확인하고 이를 refinement하는데 적극적으로 도와야 합니다. 또한 병목 현상이 있다면 해당 이슈를 해결하기 위해 노력해야 합니다. 점심 시간에는 오전 중 처리되어야 했던 업무들이 처리되었는지 혹은 오늘 중으로 처리가 되어야 하는 일이 진행되지 않은 것 같는지, 그리고 만일 놓쳐진게 있다면 리마인더를 준비하여 팀원들이 점심 식사 후 업무 복귀 전 인지할 수 있도록 합니다. 퇴근 -2시간 전에 동일한 방식으로 업무 진행에 대해 확인하고 리마인더를 진행합니다. 대신 여기에서는 연기될 가능성이 있는 업무들에 대해 중점적으로 checking을 하며 필요하다면 업무 우선순위를 정렬합니다. 추가적으로 progress를 재확인하여 프로젝트 진척도 상으로 문제 사항은 없는지 확인 합니다.
이와는 별개로 프로젝트 매니저는 모든 프로젝트 관련 정보에 대해 대기하고 이를 통합하고 정렬하여 정리하고 업데이트한 뒤 공유하는 과정(documentation)을 지속적으로 해야합니다. 만일 dependency가 팀원 사이 혹은 조직 사이 존재하는 업무가 있다면 이 부분에 대해서는 명확하게 정보를 확인하고 투명화 시키는 것이 중요합니다. 여기에 더해 만일 예상치 못한 이슈가 생성된다면 이를 주도적으로 처리해야합니다.
또한 의사결정이 생성되었다면 모두가 알 수 있도록 빠르게 공개/공유하는 것이 중요합니다. 여기에 더해 마일스톤이 달성되었거나 달성 날짜를 지키지 못하여졌다면 이를 공개공유해야합니다. 전자의 경우 호들갑스러운 축하를 후자의 경우 to teams라는 어두운 메세지의 disciplinary를 강조하는 메시지가 필요합니다.
위클리 = 팀 내부 싱크업을 위클리 단위에서 inspection & adaption 하는 방향으로 진행합니다. 그리고 의사결정자를 포함한 모든 인력들이 확인 할 수 있도록 프로젝트 진척에 대한 보고서를 작성하여 공개/공유합니다.
팀 외부와도 복잡도(dependency)가 높은 프로젝트라면 주 단위로 해당 팀들/조직들과 싱크업 회의를 진행하도록 합니다. 프로젝트 매니저는 해당 팀/조직 또한 프로젝트 관리 범위에 포함하여 관리해야 합니다. 다만 데일리 단위의 tracking은 정보 로드를 증가시키고 효율성을 저해하므로 위클리 레벨의 tracking & monitoring을 진행합니다.
바이 위클리 = Management team의 관심도가 높은 프로젝트라면 프로젝트 상황 공유를 위해 management team과 15분 ~ 30분 정도의 바이위클리 미팅 셋업이 필요합니다. 이는 빠른 의사결정을 도우면서도 management team이 project team을 신뢰할 수 있도록 돕습니다.
여기에 더해 프로젝트 팀 내부에서도 2주 단위는 중요한데, 이는 2주 단위의 increment를 리뷰하며 업무 진척도가 적정한지 그리고 우리의 일하는 방식에 있어서 개선점이 필요한지, 그리고 management team의 방향성에 대한 alignment를 진행할 수 있기 때문입니다. weekly check-ins를 확장하여 하는 방식을 추천합니다.
몬슬리 = 3개월 이상의 기간이 필요한 프로젝트에 추가합니다. 관련 부서가 정말 다양 - 전사적 관점 - 하고, management team의 관심도가 높지 않은 프로젝트라면 몬슬리에 셋업해야 하는 것들이 추가되는데, 우선 관련 부서와의 싱크업은 monthly 단위로 - 특히 타사와 같은 외부 조직 - 진행하며 management team과의 싱크업은 바이위클리가 아닌 몬슬리 단위로 진행합니다. 여기에 더해 복잡도가 대단히 높은 프로젝트라면 프로젝트 팀 대상으로 한달 단위로 increment 리뷰를 하며 향후 1달에 해야할 업무에 대한 목표 또한 수립하는 장기 관점의 sync-up 회의 수립이 필수적으로 필요합니다.
즉 프로젝트 팀은 주기에 따라 다양한 관점에서 프로젝트를 리뷰하고 회고하며 계획을 재수정하며 적용하여 프로젝트를 성공에 가깝도록 해야하며 프로젝트 매니저는 해당 계획이 잘 수행되는지 확인하며 사람들이 계획된 일정을 따를 수 있도록 최대한 지원을 해야 하며 그 사이에서 생성되거나 필요한 정보를 확인하고 이를 지속적으로 통합 분석하여 남기는 작업을 해야 합니다.
4. Finalization phase = 데일리 루틴은 필요치 않아집니다만 성과에 대한 평가 및 프로젝트 단위 관점에서의 리뷰 및 회고가 필요합니다. 리뷰 및 회고는 프로젝트 팀원 전체가 그리고 복잡도 혹은 전체 커버리지가 높았다면 프로젝트 팀 외부에서도 진행되도록 유도해야합니다. 그리고 마지막으로 프로젝트 관리자 스스로가 프로젝트 관리 관점에서 리뷰 및 회고를 진행하여 회사 내의 자산으로서 프로젝트를 전환시키는데 집중해야 합니다.

**Answer — English**
This depends on the project phase.
1. Initiative phase
> At project initiation, work should proceed by confirming the list of needed tasks and eliminating unknown points.
There is more weekly-routine work than daily-routine work; you follow up and organize regular meetings to drive consensus among stakeholders as much as possible. If needed, listen to stakeholders' opinions via 1:1s or light team meetings, and gather their needs not only about the project's goals/vision but also about how the project should be run.
2. Planning phase
> To establish the project management plan (milestones, scope, risk, communication, etc.), you must steadily hold meetings with relevant parties. As in the initiative phase, this is still closer to a set of actions to achieve a goal than something you'd call a daily routine. Even so, from now on the project manager acts on behalf of the decision-maker, so a routine of reporting project progress weekly must be added, and anything progressing slower than expected needs fast escalation.
With the project team, actively use surveys & 1:1s to confirm members' personal motivation, expectations for the project, prior experience with project management approaches, and additional needs. After completing this, actively run team-building sessions, focused on aligning the project's vision and goals while actively proposing the project management approach and collaboration style.
3. Execution phase & Monitoring phase
> Daily / weekly / bi-weekly / monthly routines run.
Daily = After arriving in the morning, before others arrive, check team members' increments and all project-related information and update everything. Organize the tasks members must do that day and arrange them before members arrive so there are no bottlenecks to starting work immediately. Also extract project progress and, based on it, secure visibility into the project's progress. Escalate if needed. If you run a daily scrum, use it to confirm the completed work and today's work scope as members perceive them, and actively help refine these. If there are bottlenecks, work to resolve them. At lunchtime, check whether tasks that should have been handled in the morning were done, or whether something that must be done today seems not to be progressing; if anything was missed, prepare a reminder so members register it after lunch before returning to work. Two hours before end of day, check progress and send reminders the same way; here, focus especially on tasks at risk of slipping, and reorder priorities if needed. Additionally, re-check progress to confirm there are no issues with the project's overall progress.
Separately, the project manager must continuously stand by for all project-related information and integrate, sort, organize, update, and share it (documentation). If any work has dependencies between members or organizations, it's important to clearly confirm and make transparent this information. On top of that, if unexpected issues arise, handle them proactively.
Also, when a decision is made, it's important to disclose/share it quickly so everyone knows. Likewise, when a milestone is achieved or its target date is missed, disclose/share it — the former calls for boisterous celebration, the latter for a darker "to teams" message emphasizing discipline.
Weekly = Run internal team sync-ups weekly in an inspection & adaptation direction. And write and disclose/share a project progress report so everyone, including the decision-maker, can review it.
For projects with high external complexity (dependencies), hold weekly sync-up meetings with the relevant teams/organizations. The project manager must manage those teams/organizations within the project management scope too. However, since daily-level tracking increases information load and harms efficiency, do tracking & monitoring at the weekly level.
Bi-weekly = For projects the management team has high interest in, set up a 15–30 minute bi-weekly meeting with the management team to share project status. This aids fast decision-making while helping the management team trust the project team.
On top of this, the two-week cadence matters inside the project team too, because you can review the two-week increment, check whether progress is appropriate and whether our ways of working need improvement, and align on the management team's direction. I recommend doing this as an expansion of the weekly check-ins.
Monthly = Add this for projects needing 3+ months. If the related departments are truly diverse — a company-wide scope — and the management team's interest is not high, additional monthly setups are needed: hold sync-ups with related departments monthly (especially external organizations like other companies), and hold the management-team sync-up monthly rather than bi-weekly. On top of this, for extremely complex projects, a long-term-perspective sync-up meeting is essential — a monthly increment review for the project team that also establishes goals for the work to be done over the coming month.
In short, the project team should, at various cadences, review and retrospect on the project from various angles, revise and apply the plan, and bring the project closer to success; the project manager should confirm the plan is being executed well, support people as much as possible in following the planned schedule, and confirm, continuously integrate, analyze, and record the information generated or needed in between.
4. Finalization phase = A daily routine becomes unnecessary, but performance evaluation and project-level review/retrospective are needed. The review/retrospective should involve the entire project team and, if complexity or overall coverage was high, be induced outside the project team as well. Finally, the project manager themselves should conduct a review/retrospective from a project-management perspective, focusing on converting the project into an asset within the company.

### Q11. When do you think is the appropriate time for a project retrospective? (프로젝트 회고의 적정 시점은 언제라고 생각하실까요?)

**Answer — English**
For a project-team-level retrospective, since memory mostly evaporates quickly, the appropriate time, if possible, is at release or once operational stability is secured after release. However, the retrospective for all stakeholders, including the decision-maker, is best done after performance measurement. This is because a high-level retrospective held when business impact is unknown can only raise unnecessary complaints and doubts about the project team's trustworthiness. Still, you cannot wait indefinitely for business impact, and a seasoned PM will deliberately push the retrospective period back and try to raise the product's performance (metrics) through fast follow-up actions. So at the very start of the project you should agree on when performance will be evaluated, and arrange for the retrospective to happen at that point too. The suggested timing is 1.5M–2M after release, and separately from the high-level retrospective, a meeting must be established where the project team can review the results of the project's performance evaluation.

**Answer — English (deepened)**
Sprint progress and project progress are the same flow read on two different time axes.
Sprint progress is the SHORT-cycle trace: the short-breath footprint expressed as completed SP / planned SP within a sprint, and as velocity.
Project progress is the LONG-arc trace: expressed as "overall %" (= done work / total scope) and "% against plan" (= done work / work planned-due-by-now — i.e., an SV-style schedule-variance read), with ETC giving feasibility.
The crux is that **predictability is THE leading indicator.** A sprint's scope predictability (how closely actual completed SP matches planned SP) is a leading indicator that rolls up into project predictability. A string of low-scope-predictability sprints predicts project schedule risk even while the project's "overall %" still looks fine. That is: when long-term (project) predictability is unknowable, you raise SHORT-term (sprint) predictability so that long-term predictability becomes knowable — the principle of unexpectancy.
You must also reconcile each sprint's increment velocity against the remaining scope (PERT/feasibility). Scope creep widens the gap between sprint completion (increment) and the project plan. When sprints close cleanly but the project's "% against plan" lags, that gap's identity is precisely scope growth.
**One concrete move:** Track scope predictability per sprint, and the project's "overall %" and "% against plan" weekly. When sprint scope predictability stays below ~90% for two or more consecutive sprints, don't judge on predictability alone — set the escalation grade by reading the "overall %" (actual progress) together with velocity:
- **Low predictability + HIGH velocity → escalate RED.** Burning through lots of work while the plan keeps breaking is fake productivity (debt) and a real schedule risk — raise it before the project's "overall %" shows the warning.
- **Low predictability + STABLE velocity → AMBER.** Don't escalate hard yet; first work with the team on how to raise predictability.
In short: falling scope predictability is the trigger; velocity and the "overall %" set the RED/AMBER grade.

하지만 이런 지표는 실제 feature level이나 프로젝트 레벨에서는 도움이 되지 않습니다. 물론 결과론적으로 모든 우리의 액션이 우리 회사의 KPI를 달성하기 위해 진행된다는 목적은 이해가능하나, 그리하여 개별 레벨에서 어떠한 결과를 내었는지를 확인 할 수 없기 때문입니다. 이는 실질적으로 그래서 이 프로젝트 이후 어떤 개선이 더 필요한지를 방해하여 고객에 대한 유효학습 기회를 날리게 합니다. 우리는 한정적 자원으로 고객의 유효학습 기회를 높여서 제품의 성공도를 빠르게 높여 나가는 과정이 필요하기 때문에 개별 프로젝트 레벨에서 허무 지표를 성공 지표로 잡는다면 이것은 분명 성숙하지 못한 관리 방식입니다. - 물론, management team은 허무 지표를 성공 지표로 제안할 것입니다. - 하여 프로젝트 매니저는 허무 지표와 행동 지표 모두를 아우르며 프로젝트의 성공 지표를 수립해야합니다. 그래야만 management team과 프로젝트 팀, 그리고 전사적 관점에서 모두가 프로젝트 성공에 대해 만족하며 인정할 수 있습니다. 누군가가 지표를 교묘하게 이용하여 - 특히, 시니어 product manager들의 경우 이런 케이스가 많습니다. - 실패를 성공으로 포장하는데 익숙하다면, 이 또한 어뷰징이며 프로젝트 팀 뿐만 아니라 회사 전체를 기만하는 결과가 될 수 있습니다. 전사적 reputation과 motivation에 부정적 영향을 줄 뿐더러 프로젝트 팀원들에 대한 시기심과 질투만 늘릴 수 있습니다. 불필요한 감정적 대립을 만들어 결국 이는 전사적 생산성의 저하로도 연결 될 수 있는 심각한 상황입니다. 오히려 프로젝트가 실패하더라도 정확한 행동 지표를 설정했다면 팀을 포함한 전사 차원에서 모든 구성원은 실패에 대해 인정하고 오히려 이를 높이기 위해 지속적으로 달려나가 고객 만족이나 제품 성공으로 갈 수 있는 동력을 얻을 수 있습니다. 그러므로 프로젝트 매니저는 프로젝트의 성공 지표를 최대한 객관적으로 검토하고 지표들의 숫자 또한 근거가 명확한지 논리적 비약성이나 허점은 없는지 지속적으로 확인해야만 합니다.

**Answer — English**
A vanity metric is the result of many actions at once, so the metric's outcome is hard to interpret as the result of any one action. Examples: revenue, cumulative downloads, MAU.
An actionable metric is one where an action's result is interpretable as the metric. Examples: cost of acquiring new users within a given period, churn rate within a given period.
Most company-wide KPIs are set as vanity metrics, because these have traditionally been the metrics investors find impressive and attractive. And it is not at all strange for such metrics to represent the whole company — inside the company many actions are taken for the product, and precisely in order to hit the company-wide KPI. So vanity metrics are meaningful at the high level.
But such metrics do not help at the actual feature level or project level. Yes, ultimately all our actions aim at the company KPI, but for that very reason you cannot tell what result was produced at the individual level. This blocks figuring out what improvement is needed after this project, throwing away the chance for validated learning about the customer. Since we must raise validated-learning opportunities with limited resources to quickly improve product success, setting a vanity metric as a success metric at the individual project level is clearly an immature way of managing — though the management team will indeed propose vanity metrics as success metrics. So the project manager must set the project's success metrics spanning both vanity and actionable metrics. Only then can the management team, the project team, and the whole-company view all be satisfied with and acknowledge the project's success. If someone cunningly uses metrics — and this is common with senior product managers — and is used to dressing up failure as success, that too is abuse, and can end up deceiving not just the project team but the whole company. It harms company-wide reputation and motivation and breeds only envy and jealousy toward project members, creating needless emotional conflict that can ultimately drag down company-wide productivity — a serious situation. Conversely, even if a project fails, if accurate actionable metrics were set, everyone across the team and company can acknowledge the failure and keep pushing to improve it, gaining momentum toward customer satisfaction or product success. Therefore the project manager must review the project's success metrics as objectively as possible, and continuously check whether the numbers have clear grounds and whether there are any logical leaps or gaps.

> ℹ️ Skill-authored (스킬 보강 문항) — not from the original 47-question interview source; written to capture the AI-assistant era. (원본 47문항이 아니라, AI 어시스턴트 시대를 담기 위해 스킬에서 보강한 문항.)

**(2) 재보정 — accuracy 말고 precision, 토대 자체를 옮긴다.**

**Answer — English**
+40% is a good signal, but reading it directly as "productivity up 40%" is risky. Velocity is a lagging indicator (the trace, Do); one month is a single point, not a trajectory. Locate it on 리·기·도 first — Do: velocity +40% (a snapshot). Li: the "1 ticket ≈ 1 SP ≈ 1 MD" convergence assumption is shaken (the same 1 SP is no longer the old 1 SP). Gi: code ships faster, but the numbers don't show review/QA/ops load or **per-person morale**.

**(1) Verify — lead with the leading indicator, and look at the distribution.**
- Don't verify velocity itself; look at the **scope predictability (≥~90%)** beneath it. If SP is unchanged but output rose 40%, either the estimation unit inflated (fake productivity / debt) or it genuinely got faster. Pull 3 sprints before and after for predictability, fragmentation index (~2.0), ops ratio (~25–35%), and workload STDEV to tell which.
- **A team average hides per-person gaps.** Measure before/after for each person — did everyone rise, or only some? For anyone feeling relative deprivation ("AI made me a low performer") — those whose gain is below average — actively run AI-native coaching (proximity: 1:1s and a health check on psychological safety and cognitive/contextual load).

**(2) Recalibrate — precision over accuracy, and shift the substrate itself.**

## Data & Logic

### Q28. Explain correlation and causation. (Correlation과 Causation을 설명해주세요.)
**Category (카테고리):** Data/Logic · **Level (레벨):** Lv.3

**Answer — 한국어**
Correlation은 regression analysis 기반으로 단순하게 데이터 사이의 상관도를 분석하는 것입니다. 다만 높은 상관도는 데이터 사이의 높은 관련성을 의미하지만 각각의 데이터의 변화가 다른 쪽에 영향을 주는 강한 원인이라고 할 수는 없습니다. 이는 측정된 데이터 사이의 관계성만을 분석한 것이기 때문입니다. 예를 들어, 도로의 제한속도와 사망률이 사이의 correlation이 매우 강한 것으로 분석되었다고 해봅시다. 예를 들어 제한속도를 10Km/h로 낮출 수록 사망률이 50% 감소한다.라고 회귀분석을 통해 강한 correlation 결과를 얻었다고 합시다. 하여, 모든 도심 내 도로의 제한 속도를 70 km/h -> 50 km/h로 낮추었습니다. 그러나 이러한 방안이 사망률을 줄일 가능성은 알 수 없습니다. 이는 correlation 분석적 관점의 한계를 명확하게 보여줍니다. 즉, 제한 속도와 사망률 사이의 correlation은 원인과 결과로 이해할 수 없습니다. 그러나 다중 도메인 그리고 특히나 big data의 시각에서 유효한 관계성을 분석하여 이를 통해 prediction이나 interpolation(deep learning 포함)에 사용할 수는 있습니다. 다만, 지금 설명한 포인트에서 우리에게 필요한 것은 causation 컨셉입니다. Causation은 원인과 결과를 분석합니다. 특히나 counterfactual를 기반으로 한 다양한 분석 기법 randomized experience에서 cosi-experience, regression analysis, 최근 노벨경제학상을 탄 instrumental variable까지 모든 분석은 원인을 파악하고 이를 통해 결과값의 통제성을 확인합니다. 즉, 이런 관점에서 제한 속도의 감소가 사망률을 낮추는 원인이 될 수도 되지 않을 수도 있습니다. Project Management에서 이 관점이 중요한 이유는 다양한 프로젝트 진행 중 발생한/발견된 데이터들을 기준으로 strong correlation을 causation이라고 오판해서는 안되기 때문입니다. 특히 문제를 해결하기 위해서 원인 파악에 있어서 필요한 것은 다양한 causation 기법이지 correlation 분석은 가장 low level임을 - 그럼에도 가장 편리한 방식임을 - 인지해야 합니다. 이는 역시나 리소스 기반으로 가능한 한계 안에서 최선의 방식을 선택하기 위한 trade-off 관점에서 봐야합니다. 여기에 더해 프로젝트의 성공 즉, 목표를 설정할 때도 이러한 관점은 매우 주요합니다. 특히나 개별 목표에 대한 지표를 설정하는데 있어서 기존의 correlation 혹은 전사적 KPI에만 매몰하여 실질적으로 필요한 actionable metrics을 놓칠수 있습니다. Project manager는 이러한 시각을 강화하고 이를 기반으로 전체 프로젝트 팀이 적정한 방향성을 수립할 수 있도록 지속 코칭해야할 의무가 있습니다.

**Answer — English**
Correlation, based on regression analysis, simply analyzes the degree of association between data. High correlation means high association between the data, but it does not let you say that a change in one is a strong cause affecting the other — because it has only analyzed the relationship between the measured data. For example, suppose the correlation between a road's speed limit and the fatality rate is analyzed as very strong — say regression yields a strong correlation that lowering the limit by 10 km/h reduces fatalities by 50%. So you lower the speed limit on all inner-city roads from 70 km/h to 50 km/h. Yet whether this measure will actually reduce fatalities is unknown. This clearly shows the limit of the correlation-analysis view: the correlation between speed limit and fatality rate cannot be understood as cause and effect. However, from a multi-domain and especially big-data perspective, you can analyze valid relationships and use them for prediction or interpolation (including deep learning). But at the point being discussed, what we need is the concept of causation. Causation analyzes cause and effect. In particular, every technique built on counterfactuals — from randomized experiments to quasi-experiments, regression analysis, and the recently Nobel-winning instrumental variable — identifies the cause and thereby checks the controllability of the outcome. From this view, reducing the speed limit may or may not be the cause of lowering fatalities. This view matters in project management because you must not misjudge a strong correlation as causation based on data that arises/is found during a project. Especially for problem-solving, what is needed for identifying causes is the various causation techniques; recognize that correlation analysis is the lowest level — yet also the most convenient. This again should be seen through a trade-off lens of choosing the best method within resource limits. Moreover, this view is very important when setting project success, i.e., goals. In particular, when setting metrics for individual goals, fixating only on existing correlation or the company-wide KPI can make you miss the actionable metrics you actually need. The project manager has a duty to strengthen this view and continuously coach the whole project team to set the right direction on that basis.

**Answer — English**
The two ways of expressing correctness are accuracy and precision. Accuracy is the view of how close we get to the result we want; precision is the view of how consistently the result we want occurs. Simply put, think of a tennis serve. Say our goal is to land the serve on the boundary of the opponent's court. Accuracy is the distance of how close the actual serve landed to the line. If you land it on the line, that is 100% accurate; the farther from the line, the lower the accuracy. Precision is how many of 100 serves landed on the boundary — i.e., whether the result we want is repeatedly produced. These two views matter in project management because they must be used everywhere estimation is needed. Budget, Time, and Resource are the classic cases needing both concepts. Since no prediction's accuracy can be perfect, accuracy — how close to perfect we get — is very important. But most companies fixate on accuracy and miss precision. Precision matters because even when prediction accuracy is low, by continuously running the same logic efficiently — fast and periodically — you can manage prediction history from a cohort perspective. That is, rather than spending resources raising estimate accuracy from 90% to 91%, set it at 90%, make fast decisions, and check the results — this raises decision-making utility and lets you properly analyze the decision framework's performance. Of course raising accuracy is absolutely necessary. But once a certain level is met, the right approach is to periodically (e.g., once every 6 months) analyze decisions/estimates made on the existing logic and update the logic to raise it. This is also very important when coaching work estimation. Many practitioners get confused using story points. Estimation logic that folds in not just work amount but also difficulty and uncontrollable factors, plus an unclear guide — deliberately incomplete, yes, because we want the team to build it together by consensus — can throw the team into chaos all at once. No matter how much/often you do agile poker, this is work that requires absolute, physical "time." So introduce these two concepts of correctness to the team, and share and agree on the initially targeted accuracy. For a low-agility team, even 60% accuracy is fine. What matters afterward is precision. By estimating continuously with the same method, raise the team's productivity predictability. Because if accuracy is 60% but precision is near 100%, the project manager can strongly correct the estimate results and obtain results near 100% accuracy. A predictable failure is better than an unpredictable success! So the project manager should focus on and coach raising precision — i.e., everyone continuously sharing and maintaining similar logic. And importantly — though it may violate the Scrum Guide — to keep precision high, you must not change the estimation logic too often. I consider about 6 months the right cadence, because only then can accuracy for future projects' estimates be secured at a fairly high level. In short, knowing something imperfect — low quality — is far better than knowing nothing at all.

**Answer — English**
Deduction can be seen as a chain of truth — continuously linking true -> true -> true and accepting it as truth. Induction, by contrast, accepts only what is experienced and observed as truth. The two seem a little different, but ultimately you can see it as expanding truth by continuously linking, via deduction, the truths discovered by induction. The problem here, though, is that both deduction and induction are modes of thinking that use truth preservation. That is, since they are closer to linking already-discovered truths, they are perfect for interpolation but have no extrapolation function. This is where the uniqueness of abduction — hypothetical inference — is proven. Abduction is the inference mode humans named last, discovered in the 19th century. Abduction is a way of building a hypothesis from a few imperfect clues — for example, the method Sherlock Holmes uses to find the culprit. Because abduction is not based on truth and instead establishes a "hypothesis," it is a mode of truth expansion rather than truth preservation. It is the inference mode indispensable for establishing truths that did not exist before: ultimately we must build products to make profit — the company's ultimate goal — and to build good products we set various hypotheses and improve the product by validating them, for which this inference mode is essential. From the result of a validated hypothesis — i.e., from induction — we again link to existing truth via deduction, then set a new hypothesis and expand further. In short, it is the very core of modern product development. The thing that explains this well is Eric Ries's Lean Startup — rapidly running the build–measure–plan loop and pivoting.

Especially in the hands of someone with little experience, it can be the most dangerous framework. So I guide borrowing XP's pair-programming concept — having a senior and a junior work as a pair. The senior is likely stuck in fixed knowledge and logic, so the junior's unbiased ideas are needed; the junior, due to inexperience, may make many errors in the logic itself, which the senior can correct.

**Answer — English**
Radical candor means complete candor, and it's also the title of the book describing the feedback strategy used by Silicon Valley managers. On the two axes of "care personally" and "challenge directly," radical candor is feedback delivered with both high personal care AND high direct challenge, aimed at the individual's growth. Unlike the conventional approach of polished feedback, it is decisive and precise to the point that the receiver feels the sting. The caution: don't confuse uncomfortable attack with radical candor. The difference is whether care for the person is high — the receiver must completely trust the feedback's utility, believing it helps their growth. That is, the feedback itself is sharp and blunt, but it requires trust that "if I accept this feedback, I will absolutely grow." When radical candor works well, the team and product gain the engine to grow exponentially.

The environment needed: first, all members must deeply understand the concept — how important an asset the feedback we exchange is, that the feedback loop is systematically established, that it operates at the right moments, and that it's a bridgehead toward precisely the target goal. Deep agreement on the hard-skill/knowledge side matters, but ultimately forming psychological safety is key. For someone to give what might look like aggressive feedback and have the receiver never take it emotionally — okay, sometimes for a while, but without separate explanation or excuse, not misunderstanding it — and instead reflect it precisely into improving their own competence or deliverables, connecting it to constructive results, both people must feel psychologically safe in believing this feedback has utility and that "I can do it and so can others." But psychological safety doesn't appear overnight. So I tend to recommend adopting agile. **Answer — English (deepened)**
- Maslow's hierarchy of needs — human needs in five tiers (physiological → safety → social/belonging → esteem → self-actualization); motivation moves to a higher need only once lower needs are met.
- Herzberg's motivation-hygiene (two-factor) theory — satisfaction and dissatisfaction are driven by different sets of factors, not a single axis (deepened below).
- McClelland's achievement-motivation theory — motivation breaks into three needs: achievement, power, and affiliation; since the dominant need differs by individual, the motivation strategy must differ too.
- Vroom's expectancy theory — Motivation = Expectancy (effort→performance) × Instrumentality (performance→reward) × Valence (value of the reward); if any one term is 0, motivation is 0.
- Adams' equity theory — people compare their own input/output ratio against others, and when they sense inequity they reduce input or voice dissatisfaction.

Looking deeply at Herzberg's two-factor: he held that satisfaction and dissatisfaction are not one continuum. The opposite of dissatisfaction is not satisfaction but merely "no dissatisfaction."
- **Hygiene factors** — salary, working conditions, company policy, relationships, job security. Their absence causes strong dissatisfaction, but their presence does not by itself motivate. They only remove dissatisfaction.
- **Motivators** — achievement, recognition, the work itself, responsibility, growth/promotion. Filling these produces genuine motivation and satisfaction.

Connecting this to the skill's voice: **grow motivators through self-management and delegation (무위, minimal-interference leadership — deliberately leaving a small gap of decisions for the team to fill), and manage hygiene factors as the foundation of psychological safety.** If hygiene factors have collapsed, stimulating only motivators will never lift the wave (파동 — the team's mood and energy as data).
One concrete move: **when the health-check's "individual motivation" metric drops, first inspect the hygiene factors (compensation, work environment, relationships), then design the motivators (authority, responsibility, growth opportunities) per-person through 1:1s.** That is: diagnose hygiene factors quickly via 도 (measurable footprint), and design motivators by reading each individual's 리 (latent structure) in 1:1s.

**Answer — English**
This case splits into three:
1) Conflict between the project team and the management team
2) Conflict caused by a few individuals within the project team
3) An overall competence-shortfall problem across the project team
Let me explain each.

1) The project was likely driven by management team pushing. "Pushing" is another word for drive force, so the situation itself isn't bad, but there can be issues like a tight schedule, an unreasonable risk response, or an inappropriately structured reward scheme. Here the project manager must quickly review the existing history. Before conducting interviews, get a clear, report-based grasp of how the project ran so that informational bias doesn't form. Especially during interviews, do not psychologically turn negative toward any individuals, management team included — the management team likely has its own circumstances too. The project manager must act like an interpreter between team and management, transposing, interpreting, and explaining each side's language, never forgetting to keep an appropriate distance from both. Then identify the most significant of the above causes, discuss a hedging plan with the management team first, then gather the project team's opinions and drive consensus. The most effective method is to hold the project retrospective all together, and in it, place a mini-Olympics up front for mingling. Loosen the serious mood fully, then on a fun point (deliberate lightness) hand out a small reward (medal-shaped cookies/cupcakes), let people eat sweets to gain psychological calm, and then unfold the more serious talk. Eating sweets activates the brain's reward circuit, so people are less startled by unwelcome topics and unfold the conversation more positively. After the retrospective, prepare a dinner and project farewell party so, like a graduation, every member's effort is acknowledged. By then a shared story has formed from the retro, and the act of eating together deepens emotional intimacy and lets people understand each other's circumstances from multiple angles — recognize this, and there the leader announces gratitude, apology, and honest emotions to everyone. The project manager must contemplate how, by arranging seemingly unsystematic things in sequence, to ultimately preserve the system that is the organization — which requires considering not only the psychological but also the physiological side of humans. In addition, discuss with the management team on a separate track how to make future sponsorship more effective.

2) Negative emotions tend to spread faster and wider. We can't rule out that all members synced onto a few individuals' dissatisfaction — or conversely, that a few individuals' behavior looked bad to the others. The first key is to find those individuals. Calling problem people "problem people" is immature and can worsen things, so announce 1:1s for the whole team, then through individual interviews identify the individuals and judge the situation. If it's an emotional-syncing problem, you must create physical/psychological distance between the problem individuals and the rest. Such a barrier lets individual members find their own thinking process and recover personal self-esteem. Options include setting up a rest plan, separating them in meetings, or not keeping them in the same space. The retrospective again maximizes this: the key point in the retro is a grand celebration of each person's contribution. The project manager meticulously tracks and pre-organizes how large each member's contribution was and what the results were, prepares a certificate with a fun title for each member (e.g., "Grew-the-café-next-door Award"), encourages everyone's effort, and avoids focusing on only a few. For the problem individuals, escalate as fast as possible and discuss with the management team so their performance/reward side and coaching proceed well.

3) When competence is generally lacking and they truly "somehow pulled it off," a self-deprecating mood can produce this. With low competence, an ironic situation likely arose where they can't distinguish success from failure, and the project's success metrics may also have been unclear. Here you need a results-sharing session that clearly conveys it was a success — running it like a Post Implementation Review is fine. Such a team likely lacks experience improving through retrospectives, and the retro quality may be low. So rather than focusing on improvement-via-retro, what matters is securing a team-first mindset and setting up growth momentum so everyone grows together. Thus, even though the project has ended, guide them to define growth again and measure the results. Review it together, and make them recognize that the journey wasn't in vain, that the hectic time that passed was valuable, and that they could push through together because the team existed. This team is still early in team-building, but as project experiences accumulate it can grow explosively. So set up activities at a level matching the team's growth stage to create stable teamwork. Also actively support the management team in continuously providing appropriate coaching. Communicate actively so future projects stay at a level the team can grow into without too much pushing, supporting the team's growth.

### Q25. Explain why self-management is important in project management, and what points you weigh as key when coaching it. (Self-management이 프로젝트 관리에서 왜 중요한지 설명해 주시고 코칭 시 어떤 포인트를 주요하게 여기면서 진행할까요?)
**Category (카테고리):** People Mgmt · **Level (레벨):** Lv.4

So what's the difference between a project that succeeds and one that doesn't? Is it enough to just understand each person really well? I'd first visualize the metrics via survey, then prioritize checking what lies behind the survey through 1:1s. And of course, simultaneously analyze the work-management collaboration tool. If you use Jira, you need analysis of Jira tickets over roughly the last 2 years. Think of it as: get workload data from Jira, and get cognitive-load data from surveys and 1:1s. For contextual load, check via Jira how many multiple tasks (epic level) were handled at one time (a WIP view), and via survey how that affected each individual member. This process primarily verifies the reliability of the satisfaction results. If a negative correlation appears between two normally highly-correlated metrics (as in this question), it's one of: 1) one of the two metrics is unreliable, or 2) other causes exist between the two metrics (*dissatisfaction with management).

If these metrics are reliable, possible causes:
1. Dissatisfaction with the management team's decisions and way of working. >> Managers' lack of managerial competence → must quickly coach the managers' managerial competence.
2. Members' self-management level may be low. >> Individuals' lack of work-management competence — nice people but can't do the work → need to strengthen members' self-management.
3. Dissatisfaction with the work domain may exist. >> From a people-management view, don't take results at face value, and don't exclude the influence of some metrics caused by other factors. So conduct additional surveys and 1:1s.

If the metrics are not reliable, possible cases:
1. Both member satisfaction and way-of-working satisfaction are actually low. >> May look like a problem-free organization on the surface — most dangerous. Psychological safety isn't established, transparent communication is failing, and there are likely many work bottlenecks. Need fast escalation to the management team, and there may be a need to lower work speed for team-building — but you must convey that lowering speed is ultimately a move to raise the team's acceleration, persuading them that it leads to higher velocity.
2. Both member satisfaction and way-of-working satisfaction are actually high. >> A standard deviation among members likely exists — some perfectionist members expressed dissatisfaction with the way of working, or some members with a strong drive to improve gave low scores and pulled the average down. In this case, quickly propose a path to a higher-level process split into short-term and long-term.
3. Member satisfaction is low but way-of-working satisfaction is high. >> The second most dangerous state. The way of working has structure, but individuals can't keep up and may find it overwhelming. Need to make them aware that a high-level process not matching individuals' competence level is the worst move for lowering individual motivation (*felt as micromanaging). Coach so that a little incompleteness exists and members can establish the process themselves.

팀 전체가 동기부여가 낮아 있는 상황으로 이는 명확하게 management team의 관리 실패로 볼 수 있습니다. 팀은 아마 전체적으로 저성과를 겪었을 가능성이 크며 이로 인해 심리적 위축과 함께 실패에 대해 개개인이 책임을 전가 받았을 가능성이 큽니다. 그렇기에 전반적으로 팀원들에 대한 만족도는 높으나 동기부여가 낮을 수 있습니다. 아마 팀이 적정한 제품의 방향성을 아직 못찾았을 가능성도 존재합니다. 그러나 이러한 pivoting 과정 중, management team이 적정한 가설을 수립하지 못하거나 주먹구구 식으로 하고 싶은데로 top-down으로 진행했을 가능성이 존재합니다. 하여 의사결정에 권한이 없는 개개인이 부당한 책임을 느끼며 심리적 depression을 느꼈을 가능성이 있습니다. 다만 다행인 것은 team-first 마인드로 모두 버티고 있다는 사실입니다. 이런 경우 management team에게 제품팀과 적정한 커뮤니케이션을 할 수 있는 방식을 제안하고 우선순위 선정을 위한 체계적인 프레임워크를 수립하면 됩니다. 그리고 가장 중요한 것은 이 모든 과정에 있어서 제품팀의 개입이 필요하다는 것입니다. 팀이란 범위에 management team을 넣어서 함께 만든다.라는 생각할 수 있도록 가이드 해야합니다.

**Answer — English**
1) When the standard deviation is large
From the team's view this is a bigger problem than when it's small. Managerially, it's advantageous for a team to hold a similar psychological state. In this case especially, a few highly-motivated individuals (likely divergent-type members with good performance and a strong voice in the team) are apt to drain others' motivation. Most managers' blind spot is to give even more power to these highly-motivated people — which is a process that further dismantles the team. From a manager's view such a person is like a vampire, harvesting others' motivation to fill their own further. A product team can't run on a single person — every job role and every member must establish themselves as a shining player in their role, and if such individuals are placed at points along the product pipeline, they actually create bottlenecks. From a Theory of Constraints view — and thinking in Kanban's WIP terms — recognize that our goal is to hold an appropriate speed per process step to maintain a consistent release speed. And a feature of these high performers is large variation in work pace: high performance when motivation is high, but becoming a low performer when their energy drops or motivation suddenly falls for various reasons. From a management view, even a low performer with high predictability is far better. Also, when performance variation is large, over a long horizon (e.g., 1 year) their output is not higher than someone who steadily performs at a fixed speed (e.g., 60% performance) — they merely have impressive spikes, while on workload data they're often lower on average. So verify these things on a data basis, change the team culture so a team-first mind takes root, and these high-motivation individuals strongly need self-management coaching. Because they likely believe they're far better than others, make them face reality on a data basis, give appropriate feedback on how much physical/psychological harm members are taking because of it, find a speed suited to them, and continuously manage/supervise so they maintain it. But a feature of such people is that they're fast followers, so most change their tendency within 6 weeks of coaching.

2) When the standard deviation is small
The whole team's motivation is low, which can clearly be seen as a managerial failure of the management team. The team likely suffered overall low performance, and with that, psychological shrinkage plus likely having had responsibility for the failure shifted onto each individual. Hence overall satisfaction with team members can be high while motivation is low. The team may also not yet have found the right product direction. But during that pivoting process, the management team may have failed to set proper hypotheses, or proceeded top-down in a haphazard, do-as-they-please way. So individuals without decision authority may have felt unfair responsibility and psychological depression. The fortunate thing, though, is that everyone is holding on with a team-first mind. In this case, propose to the management team a way to communicate appropriately with the product team, and establish a systematic framework for prioritization. And most important: the product team's involvement is needed throughout this whole process. Guide them to think of "building it together" by putting the management team inside the scope of "team."

Impact (Power) / Interest (Influence)

> ℹ️ Note (보충): In practice these two axes form a 2x2 Power/Interest grid, splitting stakeholders into four quadrants — manage-closely (high power, high interest) / keep-satisfied (high power, low interest) / keep-informed (low power, high interest) / monitor (low power, low interest) — so engagement strategy can be differentiated per stakeholder.

### Q17. Explain upper management. And explain the method for succeeding at upper management and why that method works well. (Upper management에 대해 설명해주세요. 그리고 upper management를 성공하기 위한 방식과 왜 그 방식이 잘 동작하는지 설명해주세요.)
**Category (카테고리):** Stakeholder · **Level (레벨):** Lv.5

**Answer — 한국어**
Upper management란 Management team을 관리하는 지식, 방식, 기술을 의미합니다. 프로젝트 매니저는 프로젝트 진행의 책임을 가지고 있는만큼 management team 대신 프로젝트를 리딩한다라는 의미를 명확하게 인지해야 합니다. 이를 위해서 upper management는 프로젝트 성공을 달성하기 위한 주요 액션 중 하나로 stakeholder management, communication management, scope management, risk management, team management까지 프로젝트 관리 전반적인 부분과 강결합되어 있습니다. 다만 가장 중요한 점은 management team은 프로젝트의 주요 이해관계자이자 고객 중 하나라는 것을 인식하고 모호한 부분을 줄이고 합의점을 명확화하여 프로젝트의 실행 단계 전에 대부분 명문화(*매트릭스화)를 해야한다는 것입니다. 매트릭스의 경우 가장 주요한 것은 engagement matrix를 공식화하고 프로젝트의 RAG 플래그 결정을 위한 index를 수립하고 어떤 조건일 때 R/A/G인지를 합의해 놓아야 합니다.
이를 위한 성공적인 방법은 Cognitive Alignment(복제 전략)로, 의사결정자와 높은 frequency를 가지고 사고 방식에 대한 싱크를 맞추어 의사결정자의 사고구조와 프로젝트 매니저의 사고구조를 얼라인먼트를 맞추어 두어야 한다는 것입니다. 프로젝트에서 결정해야하는 다양한 주제에 대해 심도있는 논의와 로직에 대한 확인을 하면서 thinking structure를 최대한 빠르게 맞추어야 합니다. 이 작업은 max 8주 안에 끝내야 하는 작업으로 최대한 2주안에 끝내는 것을 가이드 합니다. 그리하여 의사결정자와 지구 반대 편에 있어도 모든 문제 사항에 대해 동일한 답변 그리고 해당 답변을 선택한 동일한 로직을 내놓아야 합니다. 그래야 프로젝트 매니저의 모든 의사결정에 있어 의사결정자가 async 모드에서 이해하고 공감할 수 있기에 리더십의 서포트를 강하게 받을 수 있습니다. 이 부분이 단단해야 프로젝트 매니저가 프로젝트 리딩에 있어서 더욱 강한 드라이브 포스를 얻을 수 있고 팀원들의 신뢰성을 구성할 수 있습니다.
인간은 지능이 높을 수록 걱정과 고민이 더 높아지기 때문에 지속적인 의구심은 의사결정자가 지속적으로 가질 수 밖에 없습니다. 이것은 인간이기에 겪어야 하는 프로젝트 환경 중 하나입니다. 그러하기에 위와 같은 사고방식의 복제와 이에 대한 양측의 합의가 프로젝트 관리에 있어서 프로젝트 매니저의 자율독립성을 유지하면서도 의구심을 줄여나갈 수 있습니다. 물론, 의구심은 결국 working product와 진척도 보고의 sync-up 주기를 잘 조정하면서 추가적으로 줄일 수 있도록 해야합니다. 그리고 이런 전략은 결국 프로젝트 의사결정의 속도를 획기적으로 빠르게 합니다.

**Answer — English**
Upper management means the knowledge, methods, and skills for managing the management team. As the project manager holds responsibility for running the project, they must clearly recognize that this means leading the project on behalf of the management team. To this end, upper management is one of the key actions for achieving project success and is tightly coupled with the whole of project management — stakeholder management, communication management, scope management, risk management, and team management. The most important point: recognize that the management team is one of the project's primary stakeholders and customers, reduce ambiguity, clarify agreement points, and document (i.e., turn into a matrix) most of it before the execution phase. For the matrix, the most critical thing is to formalize the engagement matrix, establish an index for deciding the project's RAG flag, and agree in advance on which conditions correspond to R/A/G.
The successful method for this is Cognitive Alignment (a replication strategy): with high frequency, sync your thinking pattern with the decision-maker so that the decision-maker's cognitive structure and the PM's cognitive structure are aligned. Through deep discussion of the various topics the project must decide and verification of the logic, align the thinking structure as fast as possible. This must be completed within a max of 8 weeks; the guidance is to finish within 2 weeks if possible. The goal: even if you are on the opposite side of the earth from the decision-maker, you produce the same answer to every issue and the same logic behind choosing that answer. Only then can the decision-maker understand and empathize with all of the PM's decisions in async mode, which yields strong leadership support. When this foundation is solid, the PM gains a stronger driving force in leading the project and can build the team's trust.
The more intelligent a person is, the more worry and deliberation they have, so the decision-maker will inevitably hold continuous doubt — this is one of the project conditions you must endure precisely because we are human. Therefore the above replication of thinking patterns and mutual agreement on it lets the PM maintain autonomous independence in project management while steadily reducing doubt. Of course, doubt should be further reduced by tuning the sync-up cadence between the working product and progress reporting. And ultimately this strategy dramatically speeds up project decision-making.

First, celebrate the person. Whatever the decision, it is theirs; acknowledge in front of the team the work they did holding a critical system steady. Three weeks' notice and a system that ran well are room to use the time as collaboration, not antagonism.

**Then re-read the severity.** The essence is not one person leaving — code stays in the company, and if the team absorbed their way over your time together, the departure is natural succession and a growth opportunity, not a crisis. Don't catastrophize. On 리·기·도: the leaver *is* undocumented **latent structure (리)** (architecture rules, dependencies, the "why it was built this way" judgment); the shock hits the team's **operating state (기, morale)**; the real risk is on the **footprint (도)** — predictability of the critical system is unknown once they leave.

**(1) The 3-week handoff — what, in what order.** The goal is not perfect documentation (impossible, and chasing it loses both). The goal is for the team to inherit their **judgment (리)** — like Gaudí's cathedral, you inherit the philosophy, not the hands.
- Right after celebrating, **re-estimate the whole schedule with the org head.** If there's no room, **change the spec**; if both are stuck, as a last resort borrow an outside senior — but only one who **has worked with this team before** helps (low context cost). A senior with no prior history genuinely doesn't help in a mid-project, heavy-context domain (Brooks' law).
- Week 1: don't have them write docs — have **the team write and them review daily in 1:1s.** The team reads their actual code (go-and-see) and discusses structure/framework/"why this way," and they correct it on the spot.
- Week 2: for each top-risk component they step back and **the team demos ops/incident response while they supervise.** "Handed over" is measured by how many times the team ran it in front of them, not by doc pages.
- Week 3: run a cycle without them, filling only what's stuck. Ask them to leave a handoff doc of **"what I wanted to do here + the tech debt I still regret."** On the last day, award a certificate in front of everyone — and as a "graduation test," have them run a **team seminar checking off a doc checklist**, so transfer-verification and closure happen at once.

**(2) Predictability and morale.** Be honest about predictability — **once they leave, the critical system's predictability is unknown until you run one sprint.** Don't pin a schedule to the pre-departure plan; put a 1-week loop on it and track scope predictability (≥~90% recovery), stabilizing predictability before squeezing velocity. Morale — in a breakdown the default is proximity, but **proximity ≠ alarm.** A PM running emergency 1:1s itself spreads anxiety. Model calm ("this is natural; what had to come, came") and hear fears quietly in 1:1s. Don't snap to a false-normal verdict — a mature team's calm can be genuine stability. False-normal is a *hypothesis to check via 1:1 wording*, not a default.

**(3) Structural change.** The essence is the **structure that created bus-factor = 1**, so change the structure, not the person. Strongest move: **no deploy to the critical system without a cross-team review** (constraint-driven cooperation) — turns the wall into mutual help and prevents a new single holder. Track bus-factor as a quarterly metric and drive "only-one-knows" components to zero. But don't force it as a heavy company-wide manual (무위) — guardrail the critical systems only. If the team is young and hasn't absorbed their way? Still not a crisis — frame it as a **rare career opportunity** (solving a critical-system problem = big growth) and invest in team-building; what matters is that there's a team to solve it. Finally, in an AI-native environment the code-knowledge gap keeps shrinking; the real gap to learn is **CS/ops continuity** — handling people and catching what falls through.

I've given all three branches; decide where to start from the team's actual operating state. If I name one order — before posting a job, start tomorrow morning with the team reading their code and them correcting it daily.

**Answer — English**
It is a way to quantitatively express, on a [0,1] scale via self-assessment, how confident one is about achieving the goal — i.e., how well the set KR is progressing. It is checked periodically, and because each individual assesses their own work separately from the actual visible work progress, it comprehensively evaluates many things behind the visible progress: the real state of the work, its risk level, its quality, and more. Some work progresses linearly along the time axis, but most work likely does not — most of the progress may arrive at the last moment like a quantum jump. So this method brings you closer to reality. On top of that, by having each individual evaluate the state of their work, it signals that appropriate authority and responsibility have been delegated, and provides an environment where work goals can be achieved through self-directed work. So OKR confidence level has not just the purpose of checking whether work is achievable, but many purposes:
1. It lets people internalize how to express the qualitative quantitatively. This cultivates the baseline attitude of thinking on a data basis for all work done at the company.
2. Data-expressed content becomes an objective indicator one can analyze oneself, providing an environment to analyze and retrospect on one's own work as a cohort, and acting as a warning sign during one's own work. Therefore one continuously improves the assessment method, manages the precision and accuracy of the numbers, and grows self-management skills including time management.
3. From the manager's side too, confidence level provides data points across diverse work — comparing individuals' metrics as cohorts, cross-analyzing individuals, grouping at the team level — letting warning signs be caught fast and raising goal achievement rates. Beyond that, this indicator can also affect organizational stability.
4. On top of this, for individuals with weak communication ability or low judgment ability, confidence level can be used as a trigger point to create deep-dive situations, so management-team support can be received more efficiently — functioning as one indicator for coaching strategy and for maintaining organizational health.

One caveat is how you coach whether confidence level was assessed properly. Unconditional cross-verification can block individual growth. So the manager checks whether an appropriate score was measured but must not surface it bluntly right away. Treat the first score as a baseline, project work progress or goal achievement against it, and at the next measurement point discuss whether it was measured appropriately while refining the scoring method. And help the person improve their own logic. Confidence level requires high self-management skill, so it takes a long time; the more review cycles you run, the better it gets, and you must fully believe that through this process each individual can become good enough.

**Answer — English**
DMF is a framework for decision-making, and it is also the most efficient and optimal framework for gauging the level of the management team's managerial capability. The lower the managerial capability, the more DMF varies by occasion and by situation and remains inconsistent. But as managerial capability rises, DMF gains structure, consistency, and efficiency — and a top-level manager even manages, via framework, the question of when to turn DMF off.
So an organization whose DMF is heuristic is a team still near the early stage of organizational management capability, while an organization that has converted it into an algorithm maintains a managerial capability above a certain level — and this also relates to organizational size. For a small organization, DMF can actually be heavy and a bottleneck (*under 15 people, per Dunbar's law). But as the organization grows, it becomes hard for members to get to know every individual, so you must scale up the way of working through systematic management tools like DMF. If DMF is not established properly, delegated authority likely was not properly conveyed to members either, so the team handles the urgent work in front of it but finds it hard to show much interest in higher-order problem solving or to take on harder problems.
RACI / DACI / type1 & type2 / "Honesty is the best policy" can also serve as a DMF (used complementarily with DACI/RACI).

Also, what tells us at what cadence and to what scope we sync up project progress, and which report should be run with which people, becomes the DMF. So in project management, DMF moves beyond being a decision-making framework and operates as an information-flow framework when actually running the project, establishing itself as a key framework that raises the overall project success rate.

Through this, decide whether a fast decision or a serious decision is appropriate. More important than the decision-maker's view is what's on the ground — genchi genbutsu! Establishing a process that gets the field's opinion fast and accurately is the way to keep decision efficiency at its best.

### Q32. Explain genchi genbutsu. (겐치겐부츠를 설명해주세요.)

The word "lean" originates in TPS (Toyota Production System), where "lean" means the elimination of waste (muda). ### Q43. Is Brooks' Law (adding people to a late project makes it later) still valid in an Agile environment? (Brooks' Law(지연되는 프로젝트에 인력을 추가하면 더 지연된다)가 Agile 환경에서도 유효할까요?)

**Answer — 한국어**
상황에 따라 다릅니다. 유효한 경우도 있고 아닌 경우도 있습니다. 애자일 팀 자체의 팀빌딩 성숙도 및 팀원들의 agility level이 매우 주요합니다. 둘 다 낮은 경우 브룩의 법칙은 그대로 작동합니다. 오히려 더 좋지 않은 상황으로 이끌어 갑니다. 만일 팀빌딩이 높고 agility가 낮다면, 팀의 velocity는 유지되겠지만 time management 상의 이점은 크지 않습니다. 팀은 신규 인력을 잘 받아들이고 업무 분장을 하는 것처럼 인식하겠지만 실제 신규 인력의 적응을 위한 온보딩에 리소스가 더 사용되기 때문에 팀 전체적 관점에서의 velocity가 일정 기간 늘어날 수 없습니다. 특히나 신규 인력이 애자일리티가 낮다면 이는 더 오랜 기간을 필요로 합니다. 마지막으로 양쪽다 높다면, 그리고 신규 인력의 agility가 높다면, 브룩의 법칙은 유효하지 않습니다. 팀의 지연은 이제 신규 멤버로 인해 해소가 되며 점차적으로 계획했던 업무 범위/일정으로 돌아갈 준비가 될 것입니다. 하지만, 팀이 이미 일정의 지연을 겪고 있다는 것은 인력적인 문제도 있지만 scope에 대한 tight한 리뷰가 안되었을 가능성이 큽니다. 결국 이 부분에 있어서 해결을 하지 않는다면 인력이 아무리 추가되더라도 일정/scope는 원하던 계획을 따라가기 어렵게 됩니다. 이는 PO의 역할과도 연결성이 크기 때문에 프로젝트 매니저의 입장에서는 기존 개발 진척도와 전체 스펙을 기반으로 데이터를 추출하여 PERT분석을 하고 프로젝트 출시일 달성 %를 기준으로 PO 및 의사결정자와의 alignment를 통해 정확한 문제 사항의 공유 그리고 해결 방안(scope control)에 대해 논의해야만 합니다.

**Answer — English**
It depends on the situation — sometimes valid, sometimes not. The agile team's own team-building maturity and the members' agility level are very important. When both are low, Brooks' law operates as is; it actually leads to a worse situation. If team-building is high and agility is low, the team's velocity will be maintained, but the time-management benefit isn't large. The team will perceive that it is absorbing the new hire and distributing work well, but because more resource is actually spent onboarding the new hire to adapt, the team-wide velocity can't increase for a certain period. Especially if the new hire's agility is low, this requires an even longer period. Finally, if both are high — and the new hire's agility is high — Brooks' law is not valid. The team's delay now gets resolved by the new member, and gradually it becomes ready to return to the planned scope/schedule. However, the fact that the team is already experiencing schedule delay is partly a staffing problem but more likely means the scope wasn't reviewed tightly. In the end, if this isn't solved, then no matter how many people are added, the schedule/scope will struggle to follow the desired plan. Because this is strongly connected to the PO's role, from the PM's standpoint you must extract data based on the existing development progress and the full spec, run a PERT analysis, and — on the basis of the project release-date achievement % — through alignment with the PO and decision-maker, share the exact problem and discuss the solution (scope control).

**Answer — English**
The very fact of debating whether to apply Scrum seems to mean the team's management capability is lacking. If a little over a month of total development remains, even with weekly sprints you can only run four. So rather than introducing the whole Scrum process, you should tailor and apply only the parts you need. Wanting to apply Scrum may mean work predictability is low or the feasibility of achieving the project goal is very low. To hedge this, you need each developer to manage their work (productivity) in numbers, raise the predictability of when work finishes, express the whole project's scale in numbers, and express feasibility based on productivity against remaining work. The most effective way to do this is to convert all work into tickets and express each one's workload on a same-logic scale. Doing this lets you specify the total project scale and the remaining work. With this environment built, run a 15-minute daily to check work progress — whether work is done and the to-dos. Because the dev team must immerse in development itself rather than in raising work visibility given the short remaining time, judge work-related progress only through the daily. Then at the one-week mark, measure the team's velocity, compute feasibility on that basis, and report. If, for the remaining period, it doesn't exceed 80% probability on PERT analysis, escalate and then quickly do scope hammering. You could add people, but that grows more complexity and increases the project's failure probability — the worst direction. In this situation the only path is changing the spec. Afterward, align the tasks well for fast follow-up and track them so they aren't forgotten and development continues. In practice in such cases, after switching to operations mode, fast follow-up tasks get dropped due to operations response — and the PM must not miss this. Even so, a retrospective must absolutely be done after switching to operations mode. No matter how busy and short on time it seems, do performance measurement via the retrospective and review the work — including the process — together to find improvement points and apply them going forward. If, in that process, Scrum could be a good alternative, you may consider adopting it after everyone has secured the hard skill for Scrum together.
